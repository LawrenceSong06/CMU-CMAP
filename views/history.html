Carnegie Mellon Action Project History

The Carnegie-Mellon Action Project was created in the Spring of 1968 with the object of recruiting minority students and assisting them toward graduation. (C-MAP -- Report, 1976-1978, p. 5) In Carnegie Mellon University, a predominantly whtie college, we recognized that there is a cultural difference between white and minority that confronts students when they are trying to achieve academic success. Minority students always struggle to access to the college resources and emotionally connect with other students in a white community. (Sanday, Johnson, Hamilton, Mullins, 1968, p. 1)

At first, we tried to address the problem by facilitating minority students to adabt to the environment, (Sanday, Johnson, Hamilton, Mullins, 1968, p. 1) , but then we shifted the concentration into making the environment more accessible for minority students.

The program initially emphasized on providing the students with academic assistance, and teacher- counselors are recruited to achieve this goal. The teacher counselors are working to provide minority students connections to the administrative personnel, faculty, and other students. They are also in charge of identifing students' difficulties and communicate to solve them. (Sanday, Johnson, Hamilton, Mullins, 1968, p. 6)

Later, we realized that the teacher-counselors are insufficient for helping the students. The teacher-counselors' multitute of roles in academic and social assistance have caused problems in supporting the students. The students are experiencing cognitive conflict between the teacher-counselors' supportive social role and authoritarian academic posture. Therefore, we developed a more complicated system included the advisors, counselors, and tutors. (C-MAP -- Black Students on Campus [DIGITIZED], 1970-1971, p. 4)

Over the years, we are also enhancing the ratio of minority students in engineering and science. Previously, most students in our program had enrolled in the school of the Humanities and Social Sciences and College of Fine Arts. However, there are few students in Carnegie Institue of Technology and Mellon Institute of Science. Thus, we encouraged students to study engineering and science and developed relevant resources including the August Program. The improvement of minority ratio in engineering and sciences is an important step of us towards eliminating racial imbalance in the college. (C-MAP -- Report, 1976-1978, p. 5)

Today, we are coninuing to support students both emotionally and academically, and we are still achieving our goal of reducing the impact of anticipated problems to minority students.
